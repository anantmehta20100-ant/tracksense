Do this now
📞 Call 911

Or your local emergency number. Tell them: “anaphylaxis — a severe allergic reaction.”

  1. Use epinephrine (EpiPen) immediately if one is available — inject into the middle of the outer thigh and hold it in place per the device's instructions. Don't wait to see if it gets worse; epinephrine is the first and most important treatment.
  2. Lie down and raise the legs. If breathing is hard or there's vomiting, sit up instead. Do not stand up or walk around — it can cause a dangerous drop in blood pressure.
  3. Stay with the person. Keep them calm and still and loosen any tight clothing.
  4. Give a second dose if needed. If there's no improvement after 5–15 minutes and a second auto-injector is available, use it.
  5. Note the time epinephrine was given and tell the paramedics.
  6. Go to the ER even if symptoms improve. Reactions can return hours later (a biphasic reaction).

Antihistamines (e.g. Benadryl) are not a substitute for epinephrine — they do not stop anaphylaxis.

Warning signs of anaphylaxis
Trouble breathing Throat tightness Swelling of lips / tongue / face Spreading hives Dizzy or faint Rapid pulse Vomiting Sense of doom
This is general first-aid information, not medical advice, and not a substitute for professional care. In an emergency, call your local emergency number immediately and follow the instructions on your epinephrine auto-injector and your allergist's anaphylaxis action plan.
